India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act rules have moved from draft to enforcement. If your startup collects, processes, or stores data about Indian users — and most do — the clock is ticking.

This isn't a regulatory overview. It's a practical checklist for technical founders and CTOs who need to act, not just understand.

## What changed

The DPDP Act 2023 created the framework. The 2025 Rules define the *how*: consent mechanisms, data fiduciary obligations, breach notification timelines, and cross-border transfer restrictions.

Key dates:
- **Rules notified:** February 2025
- **Grace period ends:** August 2025 (for most fiduciaries)
- **Significant Data Fiduciaries:** Additional obligations apply, list to be published

## The compliance checklist

### 1. Consent infrastructure

You must collect **explicit, granular consent** before processing personal data. Pre-checked boxes, bundled consents, and dark patterns are explicitly prohibited.

**What to build:**
- A consent management layer that records: what was consented to, when, and which version of your privacy notice was shown
- Separate consent toggles for each processing purpose (marketing ≠ analytics ≠ product improvement)
- A user-facing dashboard where users can view and withdraw consent

```typescript
// Consent record schema (minimum)
interface ConsentRecord {
  userId: string;
  purpose: string;        // "marketing" | "analytics" | "personalization"
  consentedAt: string;    // ISO 8601
  privacyPolicyVersion: string;
  ipAddress: string;      // for audit trail
  withdrawn: boolean;
  withdrawnAt?: string;
}
```

### 2. Data Principal rights

Users have the right to:
- **Access** their data (respond within 48 hours)
- **Correction** of inaccurate data
- **Erasure** (the "right to be forgotten")
- **Grievance redressal** via a named officer

**What to build:** An internal admin panel that can export all data for a given user ID, flag records for deletion, and log the response time. This is also your audit trail during an investigation.

### 3. Breach notification

You must notify the **Data Protection Board** of a breach **within 72 hours** of becoming aware of it. There is no minimum threshold — even a small breach must be reported.

**What to build:** An incident response playbook that starts with detection and ends with a filed notification. Automate the detection layer — a breach you find manually after 5 days is still a breach you should have found in 6 hours.

### 4. Cross-border transfers

Personal data can be transferred outside India only to countries on a government-approved whitelist (not yet published as of April 2026). Until the list is published:

- Audit where your data actually lives (AWS region, third-party SaaS, CDN logs)
- Review your DPAs with vendors like Stripe, Mixpanel, Intercom
- Add data residency toggles to your infrastructure if you're processing at scale

### 5. Children's data

Processing data of anyone under 18 requires **verifiable parental consent**. If your product has any path a minor could use, you need an age verification layer.

## What the fine looks like

The DPDP Act caps penalties at **₹250 crore (~$30M USD)** per instance of non-compliance. The Board can also direct you to stop processing entirely — which for a SaaS business is existential.

## Bottom line

Start with consent infrastructure and breach response. Those are the two areas where the rules are most specific and enforcement is most likely. Cross-border transfer rules will clarify once the whitelist publishes — build flexibility into your architecture now so you're not re-engineering under deadline.
